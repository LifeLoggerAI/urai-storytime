FINITE TIME — FARM TO LAKE
NARRATION CANDIDATE V2
STATUS: NOT APPROVED

Before there was water, there was land.

My mom and dad ran a dairy farm. Morning showed up whether anybody was ready or not. I was too young to see how many hands were holding that world together.

When the ground iced over, the cows still had to be milked. My mom trusted one cow enough to take hold of her tail. The cow climbed. Mom slid. They made it up together.

The weather fed the hay. The hay fed the cattle. The cattle fed the work. Nothing stood alone. I did not have words for systems. I was just a little boy living inside one.

My mom believed the house could always make room for one more person. We all got sick around that time. Then a firework bounced under the porch like the world's cheapest guided missile.

That was our family: kindness, chaos, and stories that got funnier every year. Our cat could not stop on the hallway floor. My brother had a skeleton mask that scared the hell out of me. My dad burned it.

Then there was the computer. Before Quake, my mom and I explored Myst together. It felt like a place waiting for us to understand it. She was beside me at one of the first digital doors I opened.

When my brother turned sixteen, he drove me to junior high in his red truck. Sometimes breakfast came with a game piece, and for five minutes we acted like it might change everything.

After Marine boot camp, he came home bigger, stronger, and still the same asshole. He put a dead snake in my shoe. I tried to beat his ass. I could not. I damn sure tried.

Eventually the dairy buildings disappeared. The land stayed. Somewhere inside those years, the land opened into water.

Lake O' the Pines.

We had a low white ski boat with blue along the side. My dad looked back. I held the rope.

Before I knew how fast a life could change, I knew what it felt like to be pulled forward by somebody I trusted.

For a little while, the world was sunlight, water, rope, engine—and the certainty that he would keep driving.
